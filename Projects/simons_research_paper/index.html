Introduction

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) has been prevalent for decades. Its prevalence in modern society keeps growing, and the methods of treatment keep improving. The Autism Cares Act (ACA) only took 7 months (from February to September) to go through legislation, and was one of the last bills signed during the 2016-2020 presidential term. The ACA provided $369.7 million in funding per year, from 2020 to 2024; this totals $1.848 billion in funding, all of which is delegated for the research and treatment of ASD in the US ([12] IACC, n.d.). Furthermore, the US, as a whole, currently spends more than $265 billion per year on care for individuals with Autism. This amount is even projected, by some researchers, to be as much as $1 trillion in 2050 ([39] Wolff & Piven, 2021, Predicting Autism Risk in Infancy, para. 1).


Children with ASD (CWAs) experience a great difficulty in the act of communication; it's so great that they are offered various interventions and special education services. These services are intended to level the playing field and give them a chance at `integrating` into society. About 15% of all public school students (aged 3 to 21), which is approximately 7.2 million students, receive special services from the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) ([32] NCES, 2022, para. 1). Approximately 12% of those students are diagnosed with ASD ([32] NCES, 2022, Figure 1). One can multiply the 7.2 million by the 12% to find that around 860,000 CWAs are served by IDEA. "Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is a behaviorally defined neurodevelopmental disorder associated with the presence of social-communication deficits" ([9] Ousley & Cermak, 2014, Abstract, para. 1) .


The field of study on ASD has a lot of active exploration and development. Sometimes the solution to a problem is to try something new. Perhaps Sign Language could be used to help solve some communication difficulties for some CWAs. Sign Language could help CWAs who are too sensitive to sound, CWAs who are inattentive to oral speaking, mute CWAs, and CWAs with speech impediments. Sign Language could also help CWAs who prefer to use their hands or prefer multimodal intervention over single-modality intervention.

History

"French physician Jean-Marc Gaspard Itard described Victor the Wild Boy of Aveyron, a young boy who was found after being isolated in the woods for 11 years, as being socially withdrawn in addition to having language and intellectual disabilities" ([7] Cook & Willmerdinger, 2015 C. K. Cook & A. Willmerdinger, page 1). In 1817, Itard wrote a paper about stuttering. Stuttering is not an official symptom of ASD listed in DSM-5, however, it has been associated with ASD for a long time (because stuttering is a communication-related problem). Also, crutch words like `um`, `ah`, and `you know...` are not considered stuttering ([15] Cohe, 2014, para. 1 & 2).


In 1910, Paul Eugen Bleuler, a Swiss psychiatrist, used the word 'autism' ([7] Cook & Willmerdinger, 2015 C. K. Cook & A. Willmerdinger, page 1). In 1911, he coined the word, defining it as "the most severe cases of schizophrenia". Bleuler described autistic children as having a tendency to imagine "fantasy worlds" and distract themselves from "unsatisfying realities" ([6] Evans, 2013 B. Evans, Abstract, para. 2). Hans Asperger began studying ASD around 1938. The disease that Asperger studied was called `Asperger's Syndrome`; the syndrome was reclassified as ASD of severity level 1 in DSM-5, on May 18 of 2013 ([16]Hosseini & Molla, 2023, Excerpt, para. 2). Asperger did lots of troubling studies of ASD throughout World War 2, but his work isn't academically relevant anymore ([17] Czech, 2018, Abstract: Conclusions, last para. / para. 14). In the mid-1960s, autism was redefined such that it was greatly differentiated from schizophrenia; "the autistic child has a deficiency of fantasy rather than an excess" ([6] Evans, 2013, B. Evans, Abstract, para. 2). Bonnie Evans argues that "the change in the meaning of autism" could be "part of a more general shift in Anglo-American psychiatric reasoning" ([6] Evans, 2013, B. Evans, Abstract, para. 3).


The understanding continued to evolve. However, Bruno Bettelheim developed a theory on "refrigerator mothers" ([7] Cook & Willmerdinger, 2015 C. K. Cook & A. Willmerdinger, page 2). Bettleheim claimed that ASD was caused by emotional and psychogenic factors, and blamed mothers for having poor parenting. Bettleheim tried removing the CWAs from their parents, as a method of intervention. In 1964, Bernard Rimland debunked Bettleheim's theory and proved that autism is primarily caused by genetics, not by poor parenting ([7] Cook & Willmerdinger, 2015 C. K. Cook & A. Willmerdinger, page 3). However, poor parenting (in general) does have various negative effects on children. Furthermore, when CWAs have parents who can provide the CWAs with more invention time, they tend to experience less disability ([20] Wang et al., 2022, Discussion, para. 4). "Parental, family, and caregiver support" helps children and youth cope with lots of things. This includes "adversity, especially if they encounter stigma or prejudice associated with factors such as their race/ethnicity, gender, disability, sexuality, weight or socioeconomic status" ([19] APoA, 2009, Coping with Adversity, para. 1). Around 1965, Ole Ivar Lovaas began developing a set of ideas that would eventually become Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) ([7] Cook & Willmerdinger, 2015 C. K. Cook & A. Willmerdinger, page 4). According to Edgar Carr and others, many researchers believed that CWAs could not use a system of communication until around 1969. In 1969, researchers discovered that "chimpanzees could be taught to use sign language". After that, "[a] number of clinicians wondered whether other nonverbal organisms, such as autistic children, could also be taught to sign" ([13] Carr et al., 1978, page 1). This quote shows an incorrect and pessimistic view of ASD. Researchers held a false belief about the capabilities of CWAs. CWAs are undoubtedly human and humans notoriously use at least 1 system of communication. Therefore, CWAs should not be referred to as "nonverbal organisms", and no one should be unsure about whether CWAs can use a system of communication.


In 1980, interventions became more natural. Researchers began using "child-preferred activities" and materials, "incidental teaching strategies", the "reinforcement of approximations and communicative attempts", and more direct treatment in natural contexts. ([11] Schreibman et al., 2015., Trend Towards Increasingly Naturalistic Behavioral Interventions, para. 1). All of these different strategies of intervention are forms of ABA ([11] Schreibman et al., 2015, Trend Towards Increasingly Naturalistic Behavioral Interventions, para. 2). In 1982, Laura Schreibman and others ([27] Schreibman et al., 1982, Abstract) released a paper, which described an important aspect of "Naturalistic Developmental Behavioral Interventions" (NDBIs). Schreibman et al.'s paper was about teaching CWAs to respond to the use of multiple cues at once by their therapist. Since then, some other papers have been made on NBDI. In 1987, Lovaas published a paper on his revolutionary study on autism treatment. Lovaas found that, with years of intense reinforcement, children with autism could improve, and even learn to function as members of society. This paper caused a paradigm shift away from just getting CWAs under control, and towards helping them with interventions ([10] Rogers & Vismara, 2008, Abstract, para. 2).


Then, in 1998, Andrew Wakefield and colleagues published a scientific paper, suggesting that vaccines for Rubella, Measles, and Mumps could cause autism. The paper claimed that the vaccines had thimerosal and that the thimerosal caused autism ([7] Cook & Willmerdinger, 2015 C. K. Cook & A. Willmerdinger, pages 3 & 4). The paper was taken down in 2010, and the authors admitted that their research was done incorrectly ([29] Rao & Andrade, 2011, *no section title, para. 4) and there is still no conclusive proof that vaccines cause autism ([29] Rao & Andrade, 2011, para. 2) ([45] CDC, 2022, 1st Question, para. 1).

Definition Summary

"In the past 50 years" (since 1968) the common understanding of "autism spectrum disorder (ASD) has" changed drastically. Originally, ASD was "a narrowly defined, rare disorder of childhood onset". ASD is now a "well publicized, advocated, and researched lifelong condition, recognised as fairly common and very heterogeneous" ([8] Lord et al., 2018 C. Lord et al., Introduction, para. 1). The change in understanding has caused the definition, practiced treatment, and mindset of ASD to evolve significantly throughout the past century ([30] Howlin et al., 2021, Abstract, para. 1). The DSM-5 is a well-trusted reflection of this evolution. The active and ongoing research in the field of study on ASD is also quite significant ([31] Damiano et al., (2014), Abstract, para. 1). The world's understanding of ASD will continue to evolve going into the future, and this is a reason for many people to be excited about the "future" of ASD.


According to the DSM-5, a child is diagnosed as a child with ASD (CWA) if: the CWA has "Persistent deficits in social communication and social interaction across multiple contexts". The 1st context is "social-emotional reciprocity". The 2nd context is "nonverbal communicative behaviors", such as gestures. The context listed area is "developing, maintaining, and understanding relationships" ([28] APiA, 2013, page 50). The DSM-5 summarizes the symptoms of ASD as such ([28] APiA, 2013, page xlii): the symptoms of ASD "represent a single continuum of mild to severe impairments". The impairments are "in the domains of social communication and restrictive repetitive behaviors/interests rather than being distinct disorders". The word represent is used to simplify the complexity of an individual instance of ASD. ASD is used as a plural, `Autism Spectrum Disorders` (ASDs), because each individual with ASD can have, and often does have, a unique ASD of their own. ASD is not a single problem and not just one single disorder. The `Spectrum` in `Autism Spectrum Disorder` captures a lot of different symptoms and behaviors. The DSM-5 goes into great detail in explaining how therapists should provide unique treatment for each of their unique clients with ASD. In stark contrast to ASD, Sign Language is much more specific and singular in its definition. Some early scholars claimed that "signed language" was "a universal human language", or "the native language of man" ([3] Power, 2022, Introduction, para. 2). Some people define Sign Language as "a visual communication system", which uses "gestures" and "facial expressions", to express "thoughts" and "convey meaning" ([1] Jantzen, 2011, page 1).


"[A]applied behavior analysis (ABA)," can be defined as "the science of understanding how changes in the environment affect human behavior". Ivar Lovaas was a pioneer in the study of children with autism; he and other scientists believed that most children with autism experience too much difficulty in acquiring "skills from the natural environment"; the scientists thus claimed that children with autism need reinforcement training; the scientists used terminology like "operant learning" or "discrimination paradigm", to describe the children's need for reinforcement learning. These terms are also used to describe reinforcement training for dogs and animals ([11] Schreibman et al., 2015., Historical Context, para. 1).


Infant and child development is a very important topic in the research of ASD. During the 1980s and 1990s, "research on infant and child development" increased drastically. Researchers noticed that CWAs needed help with skills that are "precursors to language development, such as joint attention". The observation that "infants are active 'hypothesis-testers' who learn by forming and testing predictions on their environments" caused researchers to realize that CWAs would benefit more from active therapy. Finally, researchers realized 3 key things. Firstly, they found that "affective exchange" (AE) was required for social motivation. Secondly, they found that CWAs had a deficit of AE. Thirdly, they found that  AE between CWAS and their therapists would benefit the CWAs significantly ([11] Schreibman et al., 2015, Early Developmental Perspectives ..., para.1).


The theory of Naturalistic Developmental Behavioral Interventions (NDBIs) was developed by Laura Scheribman and her colleagues in 2015 ([11] Schreibman et al., 2015, whole paper). NDBIs combine the ideas of ABA, AE, and the importance of early interventions. NDBIs also combine behavioral research with empirical research, thus improving the methodology behind ASD research. The most important general idea of NDBIs is that they focus on individualized therapy which is attuned to the CWA's specific case of ASD. Sign Language could create new opportunities for some CWAs to feel more engaged with their therapist. The visual aspect of Sign Language could also allow therapists to analyze CWAs from a new linguistic perspective. The new insights that could arise from the adoption of Sign Language, not just as a side hobby, but as a mainstream form of communication, could be capaciously momentous.

Sign Language Can Help

Sign Language has been accepted as an alternative to orally spoken language. Approximately "92% of young people" in the UK believe that Sign Language should even be taught in public schools ([2] Bowman-Smart et al., 2019, Introduction, para. 3). Studies have proven that infants (without ASD) can learn Sign Language, and adults can learn it too. Some studies have even shown that CWAs can learn Sign Language ([41] Hein, 2019, page 2). Considering how Sign Language has helped the deaf communicate with each other, one could hypothesize that Sign Language could help CWAs as well. There are (at least) 4 different groups of CWAs, all of which could experience benefits. A short undergraduate study from 2019 found that CWAs had improved language skills after learning Sign Language (for 2 CWAs out of a sample of 3) ([41] Hein, 2019, page 2).


Firstly, quite a few CWAs are extremely sensitive to sounds. This is typically a case of phonophobia. Sometimes the CWA has misophonia (irritability by specific sounds), hyperacusis (physical pain, caused by loud sounds) ([42] Danesh et al., 2021, Abstract, para. 1), or tinnitus (ringing in the ear) ([43] Danesh et al., 2015, Abstract, Results). Sensory issues like these occur in 80% of ASD cases([21] Pan et al., 2022, Introduction, para. 1). Secondly, some CWAs tend to pay very little or no attention to sounds. CWAs who are extremely sensitive to sound might be too frightened or even terrified for them to be able to interact with their therapist (via the oral modality). Both the over-sensitive CWAs and the sound-ignoring CWAs might benefit from the use of Sign Language. Thirdly, there are mute CWAs, who might be able to hear just fine, but rarely or never actually use the oral modality. Fourthly, there are CWAs with speech impediments, who are hard to understand, due to their inability to pronounce words correctly (or their difficulty in achieving the proper pronunciation of words). Both the mute CWAs and the CWAs with poor pronunciation skills could use Sign Language to improve their ability to communicate with their therapist(s). Ultimately, this improved ability to communicate would benefit CWAs. The accessibility of communication has a positive affect on mental health. Edgar Carr et al. also emphasized an important new idea. Carr said that one can hypothesize "that manual communication would be easier to generalize to non-treatment settings". There is no proof or evidence that the manual modality can't be used for all general purposes ([14] Nunes, 2008, page "18" (actually page 2)).


Sign Language could also be used as some kind of coping mechanism. CWAs often use a wide variety of coping mechanisms. Sign Language might be somewhat difficult to use, because the CWA would have to first learn Sign Language. Nonetheless, Sign Language avoids the use of sound, and gives the CWA a genuine reason to use their hands AND communicate at the same time. This makes Sign Language a potential coping mechanism for phonophobia (fear of loud sounds).


Responsive activities "[respond] to [the student's] preferences and needs or modes". "When students participate in a range of cognitive and social" responsive activities, they tend to learn better. Multimodal learning (MML) is very responsive. MML allows educators to "shift their emphasis from the delivery of instruction to the engagement of their learner" ([18] Bouchey et al., 2023, page 37). These facts are true of children in general, and should apply to CWAs as well.

Benefits of Sign Language

For some CWAs, after learning and using Sign Language, they might be able to learn & understand gestures better; even though gesture and Sign Language are not the same as each other. Sign Language might be especially successful as an early intervention, even though infants with deficits in learning language (who typically are diagnosed with ASD a few years later) often have a hard time learning gestures. The difference could become a benefit to the CWAs. Gesture can be taught naturally for typical children, and Sign Language can be taught via interventions for CWAs. When infants with a typical aptitude for communication learn gesture, they tend to learn speech just a few months afterward. The CWAs might be able to start developing strong communication skills, just a few months or years after learning Sign Language.


The act of learning Sign Language could have many more benefits for CWAs as well. The learning of a foreign language has been shown to improve language skills in general, for the learner. Thus, a person who learns Sign Language could be expected to see similar benefits. The current research on this is not extensive enough to make this claim certain. However, research has shown that infants benefit from learning Sign Language. ([1] Jantzen, 2011, page 5) found that infants experienced a significant benefit to their language skills, as late as 96 months after learning Sign Language. The implications of this research are truly immense (see `Beyond ASD` for more on this). For some CWAs, the act of learning Sign Language could improve their writing skills and their ability to communicate in the oral modality.


Face masks are great examples for comparing communication based on oral communication (OL) and Sign Language (SL). Face masks were used a lot during the CoViD-19 Pandemic. They cover the mouth and nose. This impedes OL by muffling sound, and impedes SL by hiding facial expressions, which are a very aspect of SL. SL users found that transparent masks make communication easier, because they don't obscure the face ([40] Gutierrez-Sigut et al., 2022, Abstract, para. 1). Research suggests that SL and OL are equally clean forms of communication for a person to use while wearing face masks.


The actual addition of Sign Language, into the clinical environments which CWAs tend to inhabit, would involve a lot of complexity. Considering that only 500,000 ([33] Dafnis, 2022, page 13) of 298 million ([36] U.S. Census Bureau, 2006, page 8, table 2) US citizens in 2006 use Sign Language, Sign Language usage is extremely scarce. In fact, the proportion of US Citizens who new Sign Language in 2006 could be interpreted as a p-value; that p-value would have a z statistic of 2.934; an IQ test is typically based on a mean of 100 and a standard deviation of 15 ([37] Grosse & Zhou, 2021, 2.1. Estimation of General Cognitive Ability, para. 2). Applying the z score, 100 + 15*z = 144 IQ. So, the percentage of Americans who know Sign Language is approximately the same as the percentage of Americans who have an IQ above 144; these 2 percentages are each approximately 0.17, or 1 in 600. One could also say that knowing Sign Language is just as rare as being a genius.

Far Reaching Implications

Signed languages can be taught to infants, just like oral languages. Sign Language might be found to be cogent at reliably improving communication skills for a large number of CWAs. In which case, one could justify the idea of trying to teach Sign Language to infants who show signs of ASD early on. Early diagnosis and treatment of ASD is preferable, and Sign Language could be used as an early treatment method. Also, an inability to learn Sign Language might provide another dimension of diagnosis for ASD. Infants who are later found to not have ASD would still benefit from learning Sign Language. A study in 2010 found that Sign Language helped infants and children in their development of language skills. "36-month-old infants [who learned and used Sign Language] scored almost a full year ahead of their [peers on average]". Even 5 years later, they still "averaged 12 points higher in IQ than their non-signing peers" ([1] Jantzen, 2011, page 5). Extrapolating this further, if the children actually chose to continue learning Sign Language throughout their childhood and life, then they would benefit from being bilingual. Finally, Sign Language might help the infants as a coping mechanism. Toddlers and other young children are commonly known to throw "temper tantrum[s]". These are often the "result of a feeling of frustration because they are not able to verbally express themselves" ([1] Jantzen, 2011, page 3). Sign Language would be a more formal alternative manner for the children to express themselves and get to move their hands around at the same time.


"Learning another language is a life goal for many" people ([2] Bowman-Smart et al., 2019, Introduction, para. 1), but Sign Languages are rarely ever taught ([2] Bowman-Smart et al., 2019, Introduction, para. 1). Old people often have difficulty learning a foreign language, but age is not the only factor that determines aptitude. Studies have shown that "neurogenetic makeup, higher-order cognitive factors, language experience, age-conditioned learning styles and motivation" are also important factors ([22] Birdsong, 2018, Conclusion, para. 4). A vast majority of hearing loss is experienced by older persons. "Age is the strongest predictor of hearing loss" ([5] NIDCD, 2021, bullet 5), and "50 percent of those who are 75 and older have disabling hearing loss" ([5] NIDCD, 2021, bullet 10).


A study in 2022 found that "In the US, there are 28 million Deaf or Hard-of-Hearing people ..." and "an estimated 500,000" of them know American Sign Language ([33] Dafnis, 2022, page 13). This means that about 1.8% of Deaf and Hard-of-Hearing adults in the US know Sign Language. Another study found that 2.8% of all adults in the USA reported sign language use ([34] Mitchell & Young, 2023, Abstract, only para.).


Learning a new language can be difficult for older persons ([4] Klimova, 2018, Foreign Language ... Older Individuals, para. 2). Older persons could benefit from learning Sign Language, but they tend to do so less often than young people do ([34] Mitchell & Young, 2023, Abstract, only para.). Fortunately, total deafness is quite rare ([44] Goman, 2016, Abstract, Results), even among older persons about 25 times as high for 70-year-olds as it is for 50-year-olds ([5] NIDCD, 2021, bullet 10). Therefore, many people could use hearing aids instead of Sign Language.


As was stated earlier in this paper, children (in general) learn more effectively in multimodal environments and from multimodal constructions (such as audio which is accompanied by visual aids). Visual aids increase the rate of learning retention for the students and cause them to be more attentive to the course content. Visual aids also improve students' understanding, help students to broaden their vocabulary, and cause the content to seem more pertinent to the students ([25] Shabiralyani et al., 2015, "Significance of the Research", bullet points).


Sign Language uses facial expressions, and therefore might allow some CWAs to feel like they are having a more personal and emotionally intensive interaction with their therapist. Furthermore, some CWAs can't stand paying attention to audio cues and work better with visual cues. Considering these small details, learning Sign Language could help CWAs learn pragmatic language too.

Interventions are more effective when the CWA/client is more engaged with the therapist ([11] Schreibman et al., 2015, Relevance and Contribution ... NDBIs, para. 1). Considering that fact, Sign Language might be more effective than the oral modality; especially when the CWA has a speech impediment or APD. There is a scarcity research that specifically focuses on the following idea; but, children have been shown to learn much more effectively when their specific strengths & weaknesses are accounted for.

Clarifications

Interventions for CWAs are more effective when given at an earlier age ([39] Wolff & Piven, 2021, Predicting Autism Risk in Infancy, para. 2). Research indicates that the ideal age to begin interventions is around 3 years, 9 months, and 22 days of age ([38] Fuller & Kaiser, 2020, Conclusions, para. 1). About 50% of CWAs are diagnosed until they are 6 years of age, and the evidence shows that they would benefit from an earlier diagnosis ([39] Wolff & Piven, 2021, Predicting Autism Risk in Infancy, para. 2). Therefore, one could argue that the idea of using Sign Language with CWAs is just a red herring. It could thus be argued that therapists should focus on more relevant ideas, such as early interventions.


Hearing aids could also be used to help CWAs, and could even work as an alternative to Sign Language. Still, though, older people are capable of learning a new language, and Sign Language, when adapted for the native culture and language, is arguably much less foreign than other orally spoken languages. Thus, Sign Language could be very useful, even though there are plenty of alternative forms of intervention.


Hearing Aids help people with partial loss of hearing, by amplifying sound waves ([35] NIDCD, 2022, What is a hearing aid, para. 2). Some hearing aids are specially designed for specific sounds and frequencies, according to the patient's specific hearing loss. Special hearing aids have been used on children with Auditory Processing Disorder (APD). In the experiment, the children were able to understand the speech of other people much better. Therefore, hearing aids could be helpful for many CWAs. Combining hearing aids with sound-canceling or dampening headphones could very well improve the communicative abilities of many CWAs. This is especially true of CWAs who are too sensitive to sound, and CWAs who are mostly dismissive towards sounds (i.e. CWAs who have a hard time paying attention to sounds).


Sign Language has 3 pitfalls. Firstly, it could be too difficult to learn. Secondly, the time spent learning it could impede on other important parts of the CWA's therapy. Finally, there is "relatively little research" on the use of Sign Language with CWAs ([26] Quinto-Pozos et al., 2014, page 90). CWAs, by definition, have difficulty with using language. So, one could reasonably expect that many CWAs might not be very receptive to Sign Language. Some mute CWAs could be equally mute after learning Sign Language as before learning Sign Language. Some CWAs might prefer the oral modality over the manual modality. Some CWAs might confuse Sign Language with gestures. There might even be some CWAs who feel threatened by Sign Language. They might state something similar to `the excessive movement of the signer's hands is too aggressive`. The research on Sign Language being used with CWAs is quite sparse, considering that many studies only focus on one subject (here, 'subject' means client / individual with ASD) ([11] Schreibman et al., 2015, Support ... Treatments, para. 1), and Sign Language is an obscure topic in general. Nonetheless, there is plenty of research for one to reasonably claim that Sign Language could significantly improve the communication and language skills of many CWAs. The research consists of mostly coincidental evidence, and some direct evidence,  but it doesn't really need a lot of direct evidence. Furthermore, attempting to teach Sign Language to the CWAs (TSLTTC) could slow down their acquisition of language in the oral modality. Also, the time and money cost of actually trying to teach Sign Language to the CWAs might not be worth the rewards. The number of CWAs who learn Sign Language might end up being ultimately insignificant. There could be extra stress and burden for the caretakers of the CWAs. This stress could reduce or nullify the benefits that the CWAs experience from learning Sign Language. Teaching Sign Language to CWAs is not guaranteed to be beneficial for all of them. Each CWA has their own specific individual limitations. Therefore, whenever a therapist (or group of therapists) is considering the act of using Sign Language with CWAs, each CWA should be tested beforehand. The test would indicate whether they are compatible with Sign Language. Thus, the act of teaching Sign Language to the CWA would be offered as a voluntary option, rather than forced as a rule. The IDEA was "designed to meet [the] unique" needs of CWAs, not force them to act normal ([23] OCR, 2021, IDEA Purpose, bullet 1). Organizations like Applied ABC are founded on the principle of offering "individualized care" ([24] Applied ABC, n.d., Our Approach, para. 1). Modern therapy for CWAs does not force CWAs to do things that they can't do, but does encourage CWAs to adapt their behaviors to the rules of society.


Conclusion

Sign Language could benefit many different groups of people. The idea that Sign Language could improve the communication abilities of CWAs has a lot of merit. Sign Language may not be for everyone, and different CWAs have very distinct and specific needs. Nonetheless, the utility of Sign Language seems pertinent and applicable to the interventions of many CWAs.


The implications of teaching Sign Language to CWAs are revolutionary. ASD is a behaviorally defined neurological disorder, and Sign Language could help CWAs by improving both their behavior and their means of communication. There genuinely is a possibility that Sign Language could foster efficient multimodal learning, and improve the cognitive abilities of CWAs. Furthermore, Sign Language is an equally plausible alternative to orally spoken language. Finally, Sign Language can be used as another strategy to better treat the unique case of ASD of each individual with ASD.
